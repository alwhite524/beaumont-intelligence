# Animal Control Performance

## Overview

Publicly available performance information for Beaumont Animal Control is more limited than the program's historical, contractual, and budget documentation.

The City publishes Animal Control workload information within broader Police Department and budget reporting, but detailed outcome measures are not consistently reported as a standalone Animal Control dataset.

This page documents the performance information currently identified while clearly distinguishing measured workload from outcomes that cannot yet be evaluated from available public records.

---

## Calls for Service

Calls for service provide the clearest publicly available measure of Animal Control workload.

### FY 2024–2025

The City reported:

- **2,428 Animal Control calls for service**

This represents approximately:

- 202 calls per month
- 47 calls per week
- 6.7 calls per day

These averages are provided only to illustrate overall workload distribution. Actual call volume varies by day and season.

---

### FY 2025–2026

The City reported:

- **2,185 Animal Control calls for service**

This represents approximately:

- 182 calls per month
- 42 calls per week
- 6.0 calls per day

---

## Year-to-Year Comparison

| Fiscal Year | Animal Control Calls |
|---|---:|
| FY 2024–2025 | 2,428 |
| FY 2025–2026 | 2,185 |

The reported difference is:

- **243 fewer calls**
- Approximately **10.0% lower** than the prior reported period

This change should not automatically be interpreted as an improvement or decline in program performance.

Call volume can be affected by many factors, including:

- Population
- Animal ownership patterns
- Seasonal conditions
- Reporting behavior
- Enforcement practices
- Changes in service availability
- Changes in how calls are classified

Additional years of comparable data would be necessary to establish a meaningful long-term trend.

## Staffing Context

Recent budget records reviewed during this investigation indicate:

- **2.0 FTE Animal Control positions**

Using calls for service only as a rough workload indicator:

### FY 2024–2025

2,428 calls divided by 2.0 FTE equals approximately:

- **1,214 calls per authorized FTE**

### FY 2025–2026

2,185 calls divided by 2.0 FTE equals approximately:

- **1,093 calls per authorized FTE**

These figures should **not** be treated as productivity measures.

They do not account for:

- Case complexity
- Investigation time
- Follow-up activity
- Administrative work
- Court or hearing preparation
- Animal transportation
- Training
- Leave
- Community outreach
- Calls involving multiple officers

They are included only to provide basic workload context.

## Police Department Integration

Animal Control operates within the Beaumont Police Department.

This means Animal Control performance should be understood as one component of the City's broader public safety operation.

Animal-related incidents may require coordination with sworn Police Department personnel when circumstances involve:

- Immediate threats to public safety
- Criminal activity
- Serious animal attacks
- Animal cruelty investigations
- Traffic hazards
- Other law enforcement concerns

The organizational relationship provides operational support that is not reflected by Animal Control call totals alone.

## Service Continuity

Available records indicate that Beaumont continued providing core Animal Control field services through several significant operational transitions.

These included:

- Changes in staffing levels
- The end of the Calimesa service agreement
- Changes involving the contracted shelter provider
- Removal of owner-surrender services from the sheltering model

Despite these changes, the core municipal Animal Control function continued operating.

It means the data has not yet been identified in the publicly available sources used for this Intelligence Center.

## Limits of Calls-for-Service Data

Calls for service are useful for measuring workload, but they are not sufficient for measuring effectiveness.

For example, a decline in calls could indicate:

- Fewer animal-related incidents

but it could also indicate:

- Changes in reporting
- Changes in service scope
- Changes in call classification

Similarly, an increase in calls could reflect increased problems or simply increased public awareness and reporting.

For that reason, calls for service should be evaluated alongside outcome measures whenever those measures become available.

## Intelligence Assessment

The available evidence establishes that Beaumont Animal Control handles a substantial recurring workload while operating with a relatively small authorized field staff.

However, the public data currently identified is better suited to measuring **activity** than **outcomes**.

Calls for service demonstrate demand for the program, but they do not independently establish effectiveness, efficiency, or service quality.

A comprehensive performance assessment will require additional outcome data.

Until that information is available, Beaumont Intelligence should distinguish carefully between:

- **What the program does**
- **How much activity is reported**
- **What resources are assigned**
- **What measurable outcomes can actually be verified**

That distinction prevents workload statistics from being presented as evidence of performance when the underlying outcome measures are not yet available.
